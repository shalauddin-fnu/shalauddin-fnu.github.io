Fundamentals of an Ultrashort Laser Pulse

An ultrashort laser pulse is a localized electromagnetic wave packet whose energy is concentrated within a short time interval. In femtosecond laser experiments, pulse durations are commonly between a few femtoseconds and several hundred femtoseconds, where \(1~\mathrm{fs}=10^{-15}~\mathrm{s}\).

Unlike continuous-wave radiation, a laser pulse must be described by both its rapidly oscillating electric field and its more slowly varying temporal envelope.

§1

Electric field of a laser pulse

A linearly polarized pulse propagating in the \(z\)-direction may be represented as

\[ E(z,t) = A(z,t) \cos\!\left( k_0z-\omega_0t+\phi_{\mathrm{CEP}} \right). \]

In this expression:

  • \(A(z,t)\) is the slowly varying pulse envelope.
  • \(k_0\) is the central wave number.
  • \(\omega_0\) is the central angular frequency.
  • \(\phi_{\mathrm{CEP}}\) is the carrier-envelope phase.

The central angular frequency, ordinary frequency, and wavelength are related by

\[ \omega_0=2\pi\nu_0, \qquad \nu_0=\frac{c}{\lambda_0}. \]

The corresponding optical period is

\[ T_0 = \frac{1}{\nu_0} = \frac{\lambda_0}{c}. \]

Example: an 800 nm pulse

For a central wavelength of \(\lambda_0=800~\mathrm{nm}\), the optical period is approximately \(T_0\approx2.67~\mathrm{fs}\). A \(30~\mathrm{fs}\) pulse therefore contains approximately eleven optical cycles. This is an estimate because the meaning of pulse duration depends on the temporal-envelope convention.

§2

Intensity and temporal envelope

The electric field oscillates rapidly between positive and negative values. Most experiments respond to the cycle-averaged intensity rather than to the instantaneous electric field. For a plane wave in a medium with refractive index \(n\),

\[ I(t) = \frac{1}{2} n\varepsilon_0c \left|A(t)\right|^2, \]

where \(\varepsilon_0\) is the vacuum permittivity.

A frequently used model is a Gaussian temporal intensity profile:

\[ I(t) = I_0 \exp\!\left[ -4\ln(2) \frac{(t-t_0)^2} {\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}^2} \right]. \]

Here:

  • \(I_0\) is the peak intensity.
  • \(t_0\) is the time at the center of the pulse.
  • \(\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}\) is the intensity full width at half maximum.

At \(t-t_0=\pm\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}/2\), the intensity is one-half of its maximum value. Pulse duration should therefore be reported together with the width convention and the assumed pulse shape.

§3

Pulse energy

The energy contained in one pulse is the time integral of the optical power:

\[ E_{\mathrm{pulse}} = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(t)\,dt. \]

For a Gaussian temporal power profile,

\[ P(t) = P_{\mathrm{peak}} \exp\!\left[ -4\ln(2) \frac{(t-t_0)^2} {\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}^2} \right]. \]

Integrating this function gives

\[ E_{\mathrm{pulse}} = P_{\mathrm{peak}} \tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{4\ln(2)}}. \]

Since \(\sqrt{\pi/[4\ln(2)]}\approx1.0645\), the Gaussian peak power is

\[ P_{\mathrm{peak}} \approx \frac{E_{\mathrm{pulse}}} {1.0645\,\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}} \approx 0.94\, \frac{E_{\mathrm{pulse}}} {\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}}. \]

The simpler expression \(P_{\mathrm{peak}}\approx E_{\mathrm{pulse}}/\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}\) is useful for rapid estimates, but it does not account for the pulse shape. The shape-dependent numerical factor should be included whenever a quantitative value is required.

§4

Repetition rate and average power

Femtosecond lasers usually emit a train of pulses at a repetition rate \(f_{\mathrm{rep}}\), the number of pulses per second. The average power of the train is the energy delivered per second:

\[ P_{\mathrm{avg}} = E_{\mathrm{pulse}}\, f_{\mathrm{rep}}. \]

Average power is what a thermal power meter reads, while pulse energy and peak power govern the strength of each individual laser–matter interaction. Two lasers with the same average power can behave very differently: an oscillator producing nanojoule pulses at tens of megahertz and an amplifier producing millijoule pulses at a kilohertz may both read a few watts.

Example: a 1 kHz amplifier

A pulse train with \(E_{\mathrm{pulse}}=2~\mathrm{mJ}\) at \(f_{\mathrm{rep}}=1~\mathrm{kHz}\) has an average power of \(P_{\mathrm{avg}} = (2\times10^{-3}~\mathrm{J}) (10^{3}~\mathrm{s^{-1}}) = 2~\mathrm{W}\). At \(10~\mathrm{kHz}\) the same pulse energy gives \(20~\mathrm{W}\).

§5

Peak power and peak intensity

Because the pulse energy is compressed into femtoseconds, the peak power can be enormous even for modest pulse energies. For a Gaussian pulse,

\[ P_{\mathrm{peak}} \approx 0.94\, \frac{E_{\mathrm{pulse}}}{\tau_{\mathrm{FWHM}}}. \]

Example: 2 mJ in 30 fs

A \(2~\mathrm{mJ}\), \(30~\mathrm{fs}\) Gaussian pulse has a peak power of approximately \(0.94\,(2\times10^{-3})/(30\times10^{-15}) \approx 6.3\times10^{10}~\mathrm{W} = 63~\mathrm{GW}\).

Peak power alone does not determine the strength of a light–matter interaction; the beam must also be localized in space. When a beam of peak power \(P_{\mathrm{peak}}\) is focused to a Gaussian spot of \(1/e^2\) radius \(w_0\), the on-axis peak intensity is

\[ I_{\mathrm{peak}} = \frac{2P_{\mathrm{peak}}}{\pi w_0^{2}}. \]

Peak intensity requires spatial information. Converting peak power into peak intensity always requires the beam size or the spatial intensity profile. Reporting an intensity without stating the beam size and profile convention is ambiguous.

§6

Time–bandwidth product

A short pulse necessarily contains a broad range of optical frequencies. The temporal duration \(\Delta t\) and spectral bandwidth \(\Delta\nu\) of a pulse are linked by a Fourier relationship. For a transform-limited Gaussian pulse, using intensity FWHM values,

\[ \Delta\nu\,\Delta t = \frac{2\ln(2)}{\pi} \approx 0.441. \]

The minimum frequency bandwidth required to support a Gaussian pulse of duration \(\Delta t\) is therefore

\[ \Delta\nu \approx \frac{0.441}{\Delta t}. \]

Near a central wavelength \(\lambda_0\), a sufficiently narrow wavelength bandwidth may be converted approximately to frequency bandwidth using

\[ \Delta\nu \approx \frac{c}{\lambda_0^2} \Delta\lambda. \]

A transform-limited pulse has the shortest duration supported by its spectral amplitude. If the frequency components acquire a nonconstant spectral phase, the pulse becomes chirped and its temporal duration increases.

\[ \mathrm{TBP} = \Delta\nu\,\Delta t \geq 0.441 \qquad \text{(Gaussian pulse)}. \]

Equality applies to a transform-limited Gaussian pulse under the stated intensity-FWHM conventions.

§7

Parameters used to describe a pulse

A useful characterization of an ultrashort pulse should include:

  • Central wavelength
  • Spectral bandwidth
  • Pulse duration and width convention
  • Temporal pulse shape
  • Pulse energy
  • Repetition rate
  • Average power
  • Spatial beam profile and beam size
  • Polarization
  • Spectral phase or chirp, when relevant

No single parameter completely characterizes an ultrashort pulse. Pulse energy, duration, spectral bandwidth, spatial profile, polarization, and phase must be considered together when interpreting a laser–matter interaction.

§

Summary

An ultrashort laser pulse consists of an optical carrier contained within a temporal envelope. Its pulse energy is the time integral of its power, while its average power depends on pulse energy and repetition rate. Peak power and peak intensity depend strongly on pulse duration and pulse shape. The temporal duration and spectral bandwidth are connected through the time–bandwidth product, and dispersion can lengthen a pulse by introducing frequency-dependent phase.

These definitions provide the foundation for discussing pulse propagation, dispersion, compression, focusing, nonlinear optical processes, and femtosecond pump-probe spectroscopy.
